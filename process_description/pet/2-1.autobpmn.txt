The process begins when a customer notices a degradation in the service they are receiving. In response, the system automatically sends a list of problem parameters to customer service. A service employee then enters a problem report into the system and compares this report to the customer's Service Level Agreement (SLA). Based on this comparison, the service employee determines the necessary countermeasures to address the issue.

Next, a decision point is reached. If the decision is affirmative, the process loops back to the beginning of the decision point. Otherwise, the process continues to another decision point where several actions can occur. The electronic service may determine the significance of the customer, or a service employee may link the issue to an extra problem fix process. Alternatively, the process can proceed without additional actions. All paths eventually converge back to the main flow.

Following this, the service employee creates a detailed problem report and sends it to Service Management. Service Management then addresses any violations of quality and investigates the problem further. Another decision point is reached, leading to two possible paths. In one path, Service Management performs an analysis and creates a trouble report. In the other path, Service Management sends the problem report to Resource Provisioning, which checks for potential issues.

Resource Provisioning then faces another decision point. It can either analyze the problem and create a trouble report or create a service execution notification. Both paths lead to Resource Provisioning sending a status report back to Service Management. Once Service Management receives the status report, it prepares the final status report.

At this stage, another decision point is encountered with three possible actions. Service Management can create a request for automatic resource restoration, which is then sent to Resource Provisioning. Resource Provisioning receives the request, tracks possible errors, undertakes necessary corrective actions, and creates a troubleshooting report. This report is sent back to Service Management, which then forwards it to customer service. Service Management also creates a problem resolution report and sends it to customer service, which receives it.

Alternatively, Service Management can send the actual service performance to customer service, which receives it. Lastly, Service Management can undertake corrective actions, create a problem resolution report, and send it to customer service, which receives it.

After these actions, the process reaches a point where two parallel activities occur. Customer service creates a report for the customer and reports an SLA violation rebate to billing and collections. Billing and collections then adjust the billing accordingly. Once these parallel activities are completed, customer service sends the report to the customer, who receives it, marking the end of the process.
